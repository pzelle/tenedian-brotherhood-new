Greek Superstitions

Traditional folk beliefs and island lore — a fully recovered page from the original site

About This Page

This page was fully recovered from the original Tenedian Brotherhood website. The superstitions and folk beliefs listed here represent traditional Greek cultural practices, many of them specific to island communities in the Aegean. They are presented here as cultural heritage, with the same spirit of curiosity and affection with which the original authors shared them.

🦇 Bat Bones

For some islanders, bat bones are considered lucky talismans to be carried or kept in the home. For others, bats are creatures to be avoided entirely. One belief is universal: never kill a bat. To do so is to invite misfortune.

🍞 Bread

Bread is considered a sacred gift from God. Village women make the sign of the cross before slicing a loaf. Bread is never thrown away — it must be consumed by living creatures. To discard bread is to show ingratitude for God's blessing.

🌵 Cactus

A cactus plant is considered essential near the front entrance of a Greek home. It serves to ward off the evil eye and negative energy before they can cross the threshold. The thorns are thought to catch and hold any evil intentions directed at the household.

🐦‍⬛ Crows

Crows are considered omens of bad news, misfortune, and death. When a crow crosses your path or catches your eye, the tradition is to say aloud: "Sto Kalo, Sto Kalo. Kala Nea na me Feris" — which translates as "Go well into the day, and bring me good news." By addressing the crow with good wishes, you attempt to redirect its omen from bad to good.

🧿 Evil Eye (Mati)

The evil eye is the most commonly discussed superstition in Greek culture, and it is taken seriously by many. The evil eye can occur unexpectedly — even from someone who intends no harm. When someone gives an overly enthusiastic compliment or stares with too much admiration, negative consequences may follow for the person being admired.

To ward off the evil eye:

• Wear blue eye charms (mati) or blue beads — the color blue is traditionally protective.
• Carry a special garlic clove blessed for this purpose.
• Say "Skorda" (garlic) quietly under your breath and spit three times on yourself — a symbolic act of purification.
• If complimented by someone you know, you may ask them to spit lightly on you, which is believed to cancel the effect of overly admiring words.

🐟 Fish

Fish are believed to be wise and deeply knowledgeable creatures. In the Greek Orthodox Church, fish is a symbol of silence — one should not speak or make noise in certain sacred moments. The Greek letters ICHTHUS (Ichthis) spell "fish" and form an acronym for "Jesus Christ, Son of God, Savior," giving the fish profound religious significance in both the ancient and early Christian world.

🧄 Garlic (Skordo)

Garlic is one of the most powerful protective forces in Greek folk tradition. Beautiful braids of garlic are hung at the entrances of shops, restaurants, and homes to ward off the evil eye, evil spirits, and demons. A single garlic head or individual cloves may be carried as a talisman in a pocket or purse.

🤲 Itchy Hand

The meaning of an itchy hand depends on which hand is affected:
• Right hand itching = you will soon be receiving money.
• Left hand itching = you will soon be giving money away.
• Both hands itching = both giving and receiving are in your near future.

The right hand has always been considered the luckier and holier hand in Greek tradition, consistent with broader Mediterranean and Christian customs.

👺 Kalikatzaroi

The Kalikatzaroi are mischievous little demons or goblins who appear in the world for the twelve days from Christmas to Epiphany (January 6). During this period, they cause all manner of mischief — riding on people's backs, extinguishing fires, souring milk, and generally disrupting household life.

To prevent Kalikatzaroi from entering your home, dip crosses in basil and holy water and sprinkle the rooms of the house. Keep the fireplace burning through the night, as they are said to enter through the chimney when the fire dies down.

According to legend, the Kalikatzaroi spend all year in the depths of the earth trying to cut down the Tree of Life, which is said to form the axis of the world. They saw and hack at it all year long, almost cutting through by Christmastime. But then they must come to the surface to cause their seasonal mischief. While they are above ground, the Tree of Life heals and regenerates — so when they return to the underworld after Epiphany, they find the tree whole again and must begin their fruitless work anew. The Kalikatzaroi arrive led by their chief, Koutsavli, riding a crippled horse. When the priest blesses the waters on Epiphany, the creatures flee back into the earth in panic.

🔪 Knives

Never hand a knife directly to another person. The proper way to pass a knife is to set it down on a surface and allow the other person to pick it up themselves. Handing a knife directly is believed to invite conflict — possibly even a fight — between the giver and receiver.

💰 Money

Money attracts money. Never allow your pockets, purse, or wallet to be completely empty — always leave at least a coin or two. Never completely empty your bank account. To give someone a new wallet or purse as a gift, always place a coin inside before presenting it, so the recipient's new wallet begins its life already containing money and thus will always attract more.

🧅 Onions

Onions are believed to have healing powers in village folklore. For a cold: grate the onion and use it as a mustard plaster on the chest — the vapors are thought to clear congestion and fight illness. For sprains and bruises: grate the onion, mix with ouzo (Greek anise-flavored spirit), apply the mixture to the affected area, and bandage overnight. Many claim this remedy works as well as any modern treatment.

🙏 Parents' Curse (Parahorisi)

The Greek Orthodox tradition places enormous value on respect for parents. Children are expected to ask their parents' blessing for major life decisions, including marriage. Disrespect toward parents is among the most shameful acts a Greek child can commit.

In Greek belief, each person has two fathers: their physical father and God. To disrespect one's parent is therefore to disrespect God. A parent's curse — spoken in a moment of true anguish over a child's actions — is believed capable of taking effect, withdrawing God's protection from that child. The most powerful and dangerous curse of all is a mother's curse, owing to the special bond formed by carrying the child in her womb.

🌱 Plants & Cuttings

To successfully root a cutting from a plant, tradition holds that it must be stolen — or at least taken discreetly. Nonchalantly snip a cutting from a desired plant without announcing your intention to the owner. A cutting taken this way, the belief goes, will root easily and thrive. One asked for openly and officially granted may stubbornly refuse to take root.

⛪ Priests

Greek Orthodox priests are held in the highest respect — people kiss their hands or their vestments as a sign of reverence. However, spotting a priest walking in the street is considered a bad omen. The recommended remedy upon seeing a priest unexpectedly is to spit three times into your shirt collar. Many also advise tying a handkerchief around something you are carrying when you see a priest in the morning, so the bad luck is "tied up" until the less consequential hours of the day.

🧂 Salt

Salt is a powerful purifying force. Throw a pinch of salt over your left shoulder to ward off demons and evil spirits lurking behind you. When moving into a new home, sprinkle salt in the corners of each room to drive away any lingering demons or negative energy.

Salt can also be used to hasten the departure of unwanted guests — sprinkle a pinch on their chair or throw some behind them as they sit. This must be done without their knowledge, or the power weakens. In some island traditions, salt must never be left uncovered at night in moonlight or starlight, as the carrier may develop warts or a rash.

👟 Shoes

Shoes lying upside down — soles facing up — are considered a very bad omen, associated with death. Never allow shoes to rest in this position. If a shoe is accidentally flipped over, right it immediately and say "Skorda" (garlic) while spitting once or twice to cancel the ill effect.

🤧 Sneezing

When you sneeze, someone somewhere is talking about you. To discover who it is: ask someone nearby to give you a three-digit number. Add the three digits together. Count down the alphabet to that position — the letter you land on is the first initial of the person speaking about you. (Example: 534 → 5+3+4=12 → L is the 12th letter → someone whose name starts with L is talking about you.)

As a precaution, whisper "Skorda" under your breath after sneezing, just to be safe.

💦 Spitting

Spitting — or a symbolic light spray — has multiple protective superstitious uses. The most common: to keep evil away when you hear of someone's misfortune and fear the same fate, spit three times on yourself ("Ftise Ston Korfo Sou" — "spit on yourself/your cleavage"). This wards off the evil. A simple symbolic spray is sufficient.

Spitting is also used before important endeavors — before going out fishing, before taking an exam, before starting a journey — to avoid jinxing the outcome.

📿 Talismans (Filahta)

Talismans called Filahta are commonly used charms pinned to children's clothing or carried in pockets. They guard against the evil eye (Baskania). These include gold crosses, medals of saints, evil eye beads, and small blue glass beads. Small cloth sachets contain mysterious contents: olive branches or basil blessed by a priest, dirt from a saint's grave, burnt candle shavings from a church altar — anything from holy ground or blessed by clergy.

The most famous talisman is the Constantinato — gold medallions commissioned by Saint Helena, mother of the Emperor Constantine. They are said to contain shavings of the True Cross mixed with gold. Jerusalem nuns and monks create particularly beautiful Filahta using holy earth and stones from the Holy Land.

🔴 Touch Red (Piase Kokkino)

When two people simultaneously say the exact same words — a moment of uncanny coincidence or ESP — it is considered an omen that they will quarrel. The remedy is immediate: both people must reach out and touch something red as quickly as possible (Piase Kokkino — "touch red"). If both touch red before either speaks again, the argument is avoided.

📅 Tuesday the 13th

While Western cultures fear Friday the 13th, in Greece the unlucky day is Tuesday — and particularly Tuesday the 13th. The reason is historical and deeply felt: on Tuesday, May 29, 1453, Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks. This was the most devastating event in Greek history — the end of the Byzantine Empire, the fall of the "City" — and it happened on a Tuesday.

As a result, many Greeks avoid starting new businesses, signing contracts, or undertaking important ventures on Tuesdays. The number 13 alone is actually considered lucky in Greek tradition; it is only the combination of Tuesday and 13 that spells trouble.

🤒 Whooping Cough (Kokitis)

Before vaccinations were available, donkey's milk was given to children infected with whooping cough. Donkey's milk was believed to contain a curative substance particularly effective against this illness, and this remedy was practiced throughout the Greek islands and mainland.

💍 Marriage Superstitions

The wedding gown: Write the names of all unmarried women you know on the underhem of the wedding gown. It is said this ensures they will all find husbands.

The wedding bed: Roll a young child on the newlyweds' bed and sprinkle it with koufeta (Jordan almonds), rice, and coins to ensure the couple's fertility and prosperity.

The bridal bouquet: Include a small pair of scissors hidden in the bride's bouquet. The scissors symbolically "cut" the evil eye, protecting the bride on her most vulnerable day.

Evil spells (maya): It is believed that a jealous person can cast a spell (maya) that prevents a wedding from taking place or prevents the consummation of the marriage. If such a spell is suspected, the couple should consult their priest immediately.

Wedding candles: Never allow anyone to take the tip of the candles used at the wedding reception. A person who takes them could use them to cast a spell on the newlyweds.

✨ Miscellaneous Beliefs

• Never chew gum at night — you are said to be chewing the bones of the dead.
• Wednesday and Friday: do not cut your nails. Sunday: do not wash your hair if you want success in life.
• Never lay your clothes inside out — you will become forgetful.
• Dreaming of someone dying means their bad qualities are dying — it is actually a good omen for them.
• If you visit someone's home, leave through the same door you entered, or any marriage arrangement you are currently involved in will fall apart.
• Say "Knock on wood, hollow the moment" to avoid tempting misfortune when speaking of good things.
• Touch a button on your clothing when a hearse passes to avoid absorbing its bad luck.
• Never look in a mirror at night — an evil spell may be cast through it.
• Count stars with your finger and you will get warts.
• Never step over a child lying on the floor — the body part you step over will stop growing. Undo the effect by immediately stepping back over the child in the opposite direction.
• When leaving a place you never wish to return to, throw a black stone behind you.
• Before a trip, fold one corner of your bedspread down before leaving — you will return safely.
• Never give a friend soap as a gift — it washes away the friendship.
• Never leave your purse on the floor — it will never be full.
