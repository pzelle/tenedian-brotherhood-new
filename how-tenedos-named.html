How Tenedos Was Named

Four thousand years of history on a small island at the mouth of the Dardanelles

The Myth of Tenes

According to mythology, Poseidon's son was a king named Kyknos who ruled in the city of Kolonai, a Miletos colony on the mainland near the island. Kyknos had a son named Tenes. When Tenes' mother died, his father remarried. The stepmother had it in for Tenes and slandered him with the help of a piper — a musician in the king's court.

King Kyknos, deceived by this conspiracy, locked Tenes in a wooden chest and threw it into the sea. The chest floated and drifted through the Dardanelles, eventually coming to rest on the shores of Leukophrys Island. Tenes escaped and made the island his home. According to the geographer Strabon, he changed the island's name from Kalydna — and some accounts say earlier still it was called Leukophrys — to Tenedos, meaning "Tenes' Island."

Later, Kyknos came to his senses and realized the injustice he had committed against his own son. He sailed to Tenedos to make amends, but Tenes was unforgiving. He severed the ropes of his father's ship with an axe and sent him away. This act gave rise to the proverbial Greek expression "Tenedian axe" — meaning a sharp, irreversible judgment. Tenes further decreed that no piper would ever set foot on his island, in memory of the musician who had helped frame him.

Pelasgian Era — c. 2000 B.C.

The earliest known inhabitants of the island were the Pelasgians, a pre-Greek people who occupied much of the Aegean world around 2000 B.C. The Pelasgians gave the island one of its earliest names, and their settlements formed the foundation upon which later Greek culture would build.

The strategic position of the island — sitting astride the entrance to the Hellespont (Dardanelles) — meant that whoever controlled Tenedos controlled trade and military access between the Aegean Sea and the Black Sea. This geographic reality would define the island's history for the next four thousand years.

Persian Conquest — c. 500 B.C.

Around 500 B.C., the Persians swept across the Ionian cities and islands of the Aegean, employing a brutal and methodical tactic that Greek historians recorded with horror. Persian forces would land on an island and then, linking hands across its full width, march from one shore to the other — a human trawl net — capturing every man, woman, and child they encountered. The Greek historian Herodotus described this technique as the "human trawl."

Tenedos, like its neighbors, fell to this Persian advance. The island would remain under Persian influence until the Greek victories of the Persian Wars in the early fifth century B.C. restored Aegean freedom — at least temporarily.

Alexander the Great — 334 B.C.

In 334 B.C., Alexander the Great crossed the Hellespont (Dardanelles) and launched his extraordinary campaign of conquest that would carry him from Macedonia to the edges of India. As he crossed into Asia Minor, Alexander consolidated control over the Aegean islands, including Tenedos, establishing Macedonian and then Hellenistic Greek dominance over the entire region.

The Hellenistic period that followed Alexander's conquests brought a flowering of Greek culture throughout the eastern Mediterranean. Tenedos, with its excellent harbor and position commanding the Dardanelles, continued to prosper as a waypoint for commerce and military operations.

Roman Rule — Last Century B.C.

As Rome expanded its dominion eastward through the last century before Christ, Tenedos came under Roman authority along with the rest of the Greek world. The island's importance as a naval and commercial station made it a valued possession. Roman rule brought the Pax Romana and integration into the vast Mediterranean trading network, though the island's Greek character and culture remained dominant.

Byzantine Era — 395 A.D. Onwards

When the Roman Empire was divided in 395 A.D., Tenedos fell within the sphere of the Eastern Roman Empire — what we now call the Byzantine Empire — centered at Constantinople. The island became an integral part of this Greek-speaking Christian empire that would endure for over a thousand years.

Emperor Justinianus I (reigned 527–565 A.D.), one of Byzantium's greatest rulers, left his mark on Tenedos by constructing large grain storage cellars on the island. These warehouses served to store grain bound for Constantinople, and Justinianus reportedly called the island "The Gate to Byzantium" — a fitting description of its role as the key to the capital's supply lines and naval communications.

Umayyad Period — 674 & 717 A.D.

The rapid expansion of Islam in the seventh and eighth centuries brought Arab fleets into the Aegean and up to the very walls of Constantinople. In 674 A.D. and again in 717 A.D., Arab forces besieged Constantinople, using Tenedos and other nearby islands as staging areas for their operations. Both sieges ultimately failed, partly due to the famous Byzantine weapon known as "Greek fire," and Tenedos returned to firm Byzantine control.

Venetian & Latin Hegemony — 1203 A.D.

The Fourth Crusade of 1203 A.D. ended in catastrophe for Byzantium. Instead of proceeding to the Holy Land, the Crusading army — heavily influenced by the Venetians — diverted to Constantinople and sacked the great city in 1204. This devastating event shattered Byzantine power and opened the Aegean to Western European domination, beginning a period of Venetian hegemony over much of the Greek world.

The Latin Empire established after the Fourth Crusade controlled Constantinople from 1204 until 1261, when the Byzantines recaptured their capital. During this period and in the mid-thirteenth century, Venetians established a colony on Tenedos, recognizing its supreme strategic value for controlling traffic through the Dardanelles.

In 1403, the Spanish traveler Ruy González de Clavijo, passing through on his way to the court of Tamerlane, noted the ruins of a castle on the island that had been built by the Genoese and Venetians — evidence of the intense Western European interest in this strategic position.

Ottoman Period — 1328 Onwards

The rising Ottoman power first touched the island in 1328–1329, when Umur Bey of the Aydinoglu clan briefly captured it. The decisive Ottoman conquest came later, under Sultan Mehmet II — the Conqueror of Constantinople.

The Ottoman admiral Hamza Bey captured Tenedos from the Venetians under Mehmet the Conqueror's authority, ending Venetian control of the island's position at the mouth of the Dardanelles. The chronicler and admiral Piri Reis (c. 1470–1554) described Bozcaada — the Turkish name for Tenedos — as the key to controlling the trade routes of the entire region.

Between 1463 and 1479, Tenedos changed hands multiple times as Ottoman and Venetian forces struggled for dominance. In 1479, the Ottoman commander Gedik Ahmet Pasha definitively hoisted the Turkish flag, rebuilt the fortress, and granted the island and its inhabitants a tax-exempt status that would endure for some time.

A census from the period of Sultan Mehmet III (reigned 1595–1603) recorded 242 Christian and 18 Muslim households on the island — demonstrating the continued predominantly Greek character of the population under Ottoman rule.

During the long Ottoman-Venice Wars of 1645–1669, the island was again contested. The Venetians occupied it and, facing Ottoman counterattack, burned much of the settlement before their forced retreat in 1661. The grand Ottoman vizier Köprülü Mehmet Pasha subsequently restored the island and its fortifications.

In 1697, a naval battle off Bozcaada resulted in an Ottoman victory under the admiral Mezomorto Hüseyin Pasha. Ottoman-Russian Wars of 1768–1774 brought Russian forces briefly to the island; Admiral Hasan Pasha retook it for the Empire. The island was again contested during the Ottoman-Russian Wars of 1806–1812, when the Russian fleet was ultimately forced to withdraw.

During the reign of Sultan Mahmud II (1808–1839), Bozcaada and its fortress were restored, and a dedicated Bozcaada Guard Unit was formed to secure this vital strategic position.

Modern Era — 1912 to 1923

During the Balkan Wars of 1912, Greek forces occupied both Imroz (Gökçeada) and Tenedos as part of the broader Greek advance in the Aegean. The Athens Treaty of 1913 left the status of the islands in dispute — the Ottoman Empire rejected the decision regarding the islands — but they remained under Greek administration.

Between 1912 and 1923, the islands served as important Allied bases during the disastrous Gallipoli campaign. The Greek population of the islands endured this period of conflict with their community and way of life largely intact.

Then came September 20, 1923 — one of the most painful dates in Tenedian memory. On that day, following the Treaty of Lausanne signed between Greece and the new Turkish Republic, the island of Tenedos (Bozcaada) was officially turned over to Turkish forces. The Greek population of Tenedos, who had lived on the island for millennia, found their homeland changed overnight.

Although the Treaty of Lausanne provided special administrative protections for the Greek populations of Tenedos and Imvros — guaranteeing their language, religion, and rights — these articles were never enforced. Over the following decades, the Greek community dwindled as families emigrated to mainland Greece, to Australia, and to the United States. It is these families whose story this website tells.
